3. ML Model + Hand-Written Congestion Avoidance Algorithm (40 points)
(a) Build a dataset. From your traces, construct feature/label pairs at each time instance:
X(t) = [goodput(t), RTT(t), loss(t), . . .],
Clearly define your feature set and any preprocessing (normalization, windowing, lag features, etc.).
The learning target is a congestion window decision i.e., predicting the next congestion window
update:
y(t) = ∆ snd cwnd
(b) Train a model. Split the dataset into train-test sets, and train a predictive model that maps
X(t) to a congestion window decision. Your model may also consider a window of the history
as input. You may use any approach (e.g., linear regression, tree-based models, neural networks,
transformers, reinforcement learning, LLM-based reasoning, etc.). Your model must be trained
using the following objective function:
η(t − 1) = goodput(t) − α · RTT(t) − β · loss(t)
η evaluates the objective corresponding to the window update obtained at t − 1, and the observed
performance at t.
Plot the snd cwnd timeseries of the dataset for a single destination including both train and test
time horizons. Separately, now starting at the test split and for each time interval, take a prediction
from your model and plot the new snd cwnd obtained from predictions (within the same plot as
the previous timeseries). Repeat this for five traces corresponding to five destinations, and plot
separately.
(c) Extract a congestion window update algorithm: Based on the predicted cwnd evolution you
observe, write down a window update algorithm. The observations must be grounded in the main
principles of network latencies, queueing, the bandwidth-delay product, and the relation between
cwnd and goodput.
Note: In-principle, a similar learned model can be used “online” to update cwnd of TCP within
the kernel e.g., eBPF from the user-space. In order to limit the scope of this assignment, we will
simply consider the offline predictions, observations and extracting a hand-written algorithm from
the observations.
